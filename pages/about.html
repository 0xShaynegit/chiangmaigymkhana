Our History

126 years of sport, community, and resilience in northern Thailand

On April 5, 1898, fourteen men gathered beneath an ancient rain tree in Chiang Mai. Teak traders, diplomats, adventurers. Their mission was simple: build a sporting club for the expatriate community of northern Siam. What they built was not just a club. It was an institution that would survive colonial boom, two world wars, and a century of transformation.

1898

The Founding

The club was formally established on April 5, 1898, with fourteen founding members and royal blessing from King Rama V. The chosen site was 90 rai of flat ground near the Ping River, then on the edge of Chiang Mai. It was ideal for cricket, polo, and horse racing.

The founding members petitioned Chao Intawaroros Suriyavongse, the Prince of Chiang Mai, for use of the land. The Prince granted it with the formal blessing of King Rama V, the deed issued for the express purpose of "encouraging sport in Northern Siam." In the same royal decree, King Rama V granted 24 rai of adjacent land to the south as the Chiang Mai Foreign Cemetery — so that the men who came to play would also have a place to rest. The two institutions were born from a single act of royal generosity. Many of the names on the club's Honour Boards are the same names found on the headstones next door.

The five founding organisations were the British Consulate, the Borneo Company Limited, the Bombay Burmah Trading Corporation, the Siam Forest Company, and the Louis T. Leonowens Company. Of the fourteen original signatories, only one held Thai nationality. The Founders

The Teak Wallahs

The founding members were men of the jungle. Employees of the Borneo Company Limited and the Bombay Burmah Trading Corporation, they felled vast teak forests across northern Siam and floated the logs downriver by elephant team. Their work was brutal, lucrative, and legendary.

Among the most famous was Louis Thomas Gunnis Leonowels, son of Anna Leonowels. A teak trader, polo player, and adventurer, Louis became one of the most colourful figures in colonial Chiang Mai. He imported polo ponies from Burma and Australia and was instrumental in organised sport in the north. His presence at the founding shaped the character of the club in its earliest years. 1920s

Literary Fame

In the 1920s, Somerset Maugham passed through Chiang Mai and recorded his impressions in The Gentleman in the Parlour. His account of the Gymkhana gave the club an international audience and established it in the wider literature of colonial Southeast Asia.

W.A.R. Wood, the British consul who arrived in Thailand in 1896 and served in Chiang Mai from 1915, also referenced the club in his memoir Consul in Paradise. Wood is buried in the Foreign Cemetery next door. His son Dick followed him as a club member for decades — between father and son, over a century of continuous membership. Today the Gymkhana appears in academic histories, travel literature, and novels exploring expatriate life in northern Thailand. The original teak clubhouse and the giant rain tree are still standing, preserved rather than renovated. Read about the literary legacy.

War and Resilience

When Japan invaded Thailand in December 1941, the Gymkhana fell silent. The grounds were abandoned. The rain tree stood through the occupation as it had stood through everything else. In 1950, returning members walked back onto the grounds and found the old pavilion still standing. They cleared the undergrowth, rolled the greens, and reopened. The club had survived.

Present Day

The Club Today

The Gymkhana sits on the same 90 rai it has always occupied, now surrounded by a Chiang Mai that has grown entirely around it. The rain tree still stands. The colonial pavilion still overlooks the grounds. The nine-hole golf course still plays as it always has.

Today the club serves a membership that spans Thai families, long-term expatriates, and visitors who come for sport, dining, and the particular atmosphere that only 126 years of history can produce. Golf, tennis, and cricket remain the heartbeat of the operation, alongside dining, weddings, and private events in one of the most distinctive settings in northern Thailand. The Club Today section of our history gives a fuller picture of where things stand.

The annual Chiang Mai International Cricket Sixes, launched in 1988, has grown into the world's best-known amateur cricket tournament. Teams arrive each year from Australia, the United Kingdom, Hong Kong, South Africa, and beyond.

Membership

Joining the Club

Membership at the Gymkhana is open to individuals and families who share a genuine interest in sport and community. The club welcomes Thai and international members. There is no waiting list and no ballot process. The application is straightforward.

Prospective members are encouraged to visit the grounds, walk the course, and speak with existing members before applying. The club office is open Monday through Friday and can arrange a tour at your convenience.
